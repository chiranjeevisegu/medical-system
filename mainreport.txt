{\fontsize{16}{20}\bfseries ABSTRACT}\\[4pt]
\rule{7cm}{0.6pt}
\end{center}
\vspace{0.4cm}

Clinical discharge summaries are the most information-dense document that a patient is given when discharged from hospital care, yet they continue to be systematically invisible to the populations they purport to describe. Written only in the language of clinical medicine - with the inclusion of ICD-10 diagnostic codes, subspecialty abbreviations, Latin pharmacological notation, and a multitude of implicit domain assumptions - discharge summaries are routinely read at a post-graduate level, rendering them too complex for a majority of adult patients to understand. The described effects are clinically meaningful: poor medication adherence, failure to attend follow-up, unnecessary health anxiety, and avoidable readmission - particularly for patients with chronic disease such as heart failure and diabetes. Although clinical natural language processing is rapidly emerging as a discipline of research, the primary focus continues to be physician-facing applications - automated ICD coding, clinical decision support, documentation assistance - rather than patients, with tools designed to enhance patients' understanding of their own medical records still in their infancy. This work contributes directly to that void.

We introduce \textit{xMaReport}: an Explainable Multi-Agent Patient-Centric Medical Report Understanding and Recommendation System --- a two-stage AI pipeline that accepts a raw MIMIC-III discharge summary as input and delivers a structured, safety-verified, plain-language patient-facing explanation as output. In Stage~1, discharge summaries are preprocessed by the \texttt{MIMICDatasetLoader}, which constructs composite reports from \texttt{NOTEEVENTS.csv},
\texttt{DIAGNOSES\_ICD.csv}, and \texttt{LABEVENTS.csv}, followed by PHI token removal via the \texttt{TextCleaner} processor. BioClinicalBERTencodes the cleaned report, and the generated 768-dimensional \texttt{CLS} embedding is fed into a linear classification head trained using class-weighted cross-entropy loss, classifying the report into one of five disease categories: Cardiovascular, Respiratory, Endocrine, Infectious, or Neurological. In Stage~2, a six-agent FLAN-T5-Largereasoning pipeline, coordinated by a
\texttt{SequentialAgentFlow} orchestrator, further processes the classified report via specialised agents responsible for clinical extraction, risk stratification, plain-language explanation, confidence-gated recommendation generation, evidence-based justification, and two-pass safety verification. The complete system is delivered with a React frontend and FastAPI backend. Safety is enforced through a confidence gate at Verification Agent~I (threshold $= 0.55$) and a final output review at Verification Agent~II, with orchestrator-level regeneration triggered upon identification of violations.

The system was evaluated on 20 real MIMIC-III discharge summaries across all five disease categories under a six-metric evaluation suite comprising classification F1, Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level (FKGL), semantic QA accuracy established via BioClinicalBERT cosine similarity, information coverage, unsafe recommendation rate, and clinical fact consistency rate. The proposed framework attains a macro-average classification F1 of $0.83$, lowers the mean FKGL from $16.3$ to $8.1$ - an $8.2$ grade-level improvement expanding readability from the top $10$--$15\%$ to the majority of the adult population - and retains $87.8\%$ of key clinical information. The Unsafe Recommendation Rate is $0.0\%$ across all test cases. Against a single-prompt FLAN-T5-Large baseline using the identical underlying model, the multi-agent architecture improves semantic QA accuracy by $12.2$ percentage points and eliminates all three safety violations observed in the baseline, demonstrating that task decomposition, specialised prompting, and layered verification - rather than model scale - are the determining factors in clinical AI safety performance.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Clinical NLP, Multi-Agent AI, BioClinicalBERT, FLAN-T5-Large, MIMIC-III, Patient Explainability, Safety Verification, Discharge Summary, Readability.
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background and Context}

Over the past 10 years, there has been an explosive growth in the amount of clinical documentation generated within modern healthcare organizations. Now, Electronic Health Records are able to hold everything from a patient's detailed history to laboratory values, imaging results, surgical notes and discharge summaries, all within a single digital record. Such documentation improves physician decision-making and continuity of care, but it creates a practical problem that is given far less attention: the reports that offer the most granularity about a patient’s health are the reports that patients are least able to read and act on. 

Discharge summaries are written by clinicians and intended for clinicians. The language of the discharge summary fulfils the need for professional communication: the use of abbreviations (such as LBBB or NSTEMI), diagnostic codes of the ICD-10, Latin pharmacological forms (e.g. \textit{per os}) and subspecialty jargon, which can be understood by a cardiologist or an intensivist but is simply gibberish to the patient it is describing. Readability analyses have consistently found the typical discharge summary to be written at a post-graduate level of literacy, far beyond what the average person is able to read \cite{Meade1992}. 

The fallout is not theoretical. Those who can’t decipher their discharge instructions are more prone to skip follow-up visits, take medicines improperly or go to unreliable websites for information \cite{Chen2024,Smith2022} Patients unable to understand their discharge instructions are at risk of missing follow-up visits, taking medications incorrectly, or obtaining information from unreliable websites \cite{Chen2024,Smith2022}. Chronic disease management studies have demonstrated strong associations with patient health literacy and readmission rates, as well as long-term control of heart failure and diabetes, with poorer-readable patients having increased rates of readmission and less optimal control of heart failure and diabetes \cite{PatientGroup2024}. To summarize: the data exists — it just isn’t getting to the patient in a way that they can apply it. 

AI, and natural language processing in particular, provides a potential way to do just that. Large transformer models trained on clinical text, e.g., BioClinicalBERT \cite{Alsentzer2019}, serve as kernels of domain knowledge for downstream clinical classification and information extraction problems. Instruction-tuned generative models like FLAN-T5-Large \cite{Wei2022} can generate well-formed and constrained natural language for structured inputs. Combined, these elements lay the technological groundwork for a system that can read a clinical report, grasp what it says, and convey it to a patient in plain language — safely, accurately, and without supplanting the physician. 

\section{Problem  Statement and Challenges }

The fundamental issue this project seeks to address is: can an AI system, given an unstructured medical discharge summary written in clinical jargon, generate a structured explanation of the condition of the patient that is accessible to the patient, along with safe, justified, and clinically grounded advice -- without usurping the role of a physician? Behind this question there are a number of technical and ethical issues that are more specific. 

\subsection{Complexity of Clinical Language }

To solve complex problems in the healthcare domain requires understanding both computational and medical principles. One discharge summary alone could have nested conditional statements for medication dosing, temporal references to previous admissions, specialist jargon from a subspecialty, and context dependent acronyms that have different meanings in different clinical settings. To translate this language requires more than replacing difficult“words”with easier ones; it requires an understanding of the clinical relationships between signs and symptoms, diagnoses and treatments, and which information a convalescing patient at home is most likely to want and need. 

\subsection{Safety and Non-Diagnostic Limitations }

Any AI that engages with patients in a healthcare environment should be functioning within tight constraints. It should not generate diagnostic conclusions - that is the duty of a licensed clinician. It can't recommend a drug or dose. It may not output information that would cause a patient to delay seeking emergency care when necessary. These are not simple constrains to enforce on a generative model, which can produce fluent, confident-sounding (but factually incorrect or clinically harmful) text \cite{Zhang2023}. 

\subsection{Interpretability and Transparency }

A stripped down version, with no indication of how confident the system is, or what clinical evidence underpins its recommendations, is of very little use to either patients or clinicians. Explainability -- revealing the reasoning behind each output -- has to be incorporated into the design of the system from the outset, rather than being tacked on later as an afterthought. 

\subsection{Information Retrieving Through Simplification }

There is always a danger of omitting the important content when simplifying a clinical text. A plain‐language report that is unreadable because it omits the key findings may be more harmful to the patient than the original impenetrable text. Fidelity to information and real readability improvement are both key engineering challenges in this class of system. 

\subsection{Effective Assessment }

Traditional NLP evaluation metrics such as BLEU or ROUGE were created for text generation tasks, and do not measure what matters in a patient-facing system (Is the explanation readable? Does it retain important clinical facts? Is it safe? Can the patient actually do something with it?). To evaluate a patient-facing AI system, a custom metric suite is needed that can directly quantify these aspects. 

\section{Importance and Motivation}

The importance of this work is based in clinical reality. The
rare-disease diagnostic odyssey, in which patients wait years to get a confirmed diagnosis, is the tail end of a far bigger
problem: patients often walk out of a hospital without being able to accurately describe their condition \cite{PatientGroup2024}. The same problem exists in the care of common chronic conditions: patients who are given clear, written information about their condition are the ones who most reliably adhere to treatment, engage with follow-up care, and have the best long-term outcomes \cite{Chen2024}.

The contents of clinical documentation could become usable for the people it described with an AI-based patient explanation system. It would be of particular usefulness in resource limited healthcare facilities, in which time for post-discharge patient education is scarce, language differences make clinical terminology more difficult to grasp, and patients are anticipated to take care of themselves at home following complex procedures.

From a scientific perspective, this work fills an important kind of void. Clinical NLP literature has concentrated mainly on physician-facing applications such as automated ICD coding \cite{Huang2019}, clinical decision support, and documentation assistance. Patient-facing applications -- tools intended to assist patients in understanding what has been written about them -- continue to be minimally studied. This work directly contributes to that new area. 

\section{Scope and Objectives}

Discharge summaries from the MIMIC-III clinical database are considered in this work. It is not designed to substitute for consultation with a physician, but rather to enable patients to better understand their care plan during the interval between discharge and the subsequent clinical visit, when they are most dependent on their own knowledge of their condition. 

The goals of the project are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\textbf{\arabic*.},itemsep=2pt]
  \item Develop a hybrid BioClinicalBERT and FLAN-T5-Large model architecture that classifies discharge summaries into disease categories and produces patient-friendly explanations.
  \item Propose a 6-agent reasoning pipeline, with dedicated agents performing clinical extraction, risk assessment, explanation, recommendation, justification and safety checking over two validation iterations.
  \item Test the method on 20 MIMIC-III discharge summaries with six measures that span classification correctness, readability, information retention, and security.
  \item Establish quantifiable safety and quality improvements of the multi-agent architecture over a single-model baseline adopting the same underlying generative model.
  \item Provide a reproducible full-stack implementation consisting of a FastAPI backend, a React frontend, and a well-documented evaluation pipeline.
\end{enumerate}

Chapter 2 reviews the related literature and highlights the research gaps addressed in this work. The system architecture, the multi-agent pipeline design, the dataset, and the complete implementation stack are described in Chapter 3. Experimental results, an analysis metric-by-metric and with the baseline are presented in Chapter 4. Chapter 5 concludes with a summary of contributions and directions for future work.

\chapter{Literature Survey}

\section{Survey} 

This chapter reviews the prior work in the following five areas, each of which directly influences the design decisions in our proposed system: summarisation of clinical text, domain-specific clinical language models, text simplification for patient facing applications, multi-agent architectures in healthcare AI, and large language model safety. The review of literature delineates the particular gaps that propel the argumentation.

\subsection{Clinical Text Summarisation with Transformers}

The use of transformer models for clinical text summarisation has dramatically increased since BERT \cite{Devlin2019} and its successors have been introduced. Zhang et al. showed that abstractive transformer models bring significant improvements in summarisation quality over extractive baselines on MIMIC-III discharge notes, and the generated summaries more effectively capture the clinical reasoning from the source document \cite{Zhang2023}. Raffel et al.\ established that a single text-to-text approach can be used for many different NLP tasks, from a single model, leveraging the conceptual basis of FLAN-T5 \cite{Raffel2020}.

These results show that modern NLP models can be relied on to summarise clinical text. However, summarisation and explanation for a patient are two different goals. A summarizer condenses the source document so that information of the original document is retained -- however the content of the summarization is also a clinical text, which is written at approximately the same level of reading with same professional jargons. A patient-oriented explanation system has to completely change the language: simpler vocabulary, abbreviated sentences and a different narrative organization (centered on patient questions, rather than clinical findings), along with explicit safety constraints on what the system can say. The summarisation literature can provide useful technical building blocks, but does not consider these extra requirements. 

\subsection{Clinical Language Models in Specific Domains}

There is a well-known case to be made for domain-specific pre-training in clinical NLP. Alsentzer et al.\ also demonstrated that BERT can be fine-tuned on clinical notes from the MIMIC-III database (referred to as BioClinicalBERT) and that this version of BERT outperforms general-domain BERT on a variety of clinical NER, relation extraction, and classification tasks \cite{Alsentzer2019}. Huang et al.\ established that downstream fine-tuned clinical BERT derivatives can be utilized in ICD code prediction based on clinical text \cite{Huang2019}. The uniformity of results over this entire body of work is that clinical text appears to exhibit unique linguistic patterns, vocabulary, and semantic relationships to the extent that it is not merely advantageous, but actually required, that domain specific pre‐training be performed if clinical NLP performance of any high quality is to be expected.

This in turn directly justifies the selection of BioClinicalBERT as the classification backbone in the proposed framework. A general-domain encoder should not be expected to capture all the nuances in discharge summary language. BioClinicalBERT's training on the MIMIC-III dataset brings the embedding space closer to the exact nature of the text the model will be dealing with. 

\subsection{Large Language  Models for Medical Q&A}

Singhal et al.\ introduced Med-PaLM \cite{Singhal2023}, an instruction-tuned large language model attaining near-expert performance on medical licensing exam questions. This finding established that large-scale trained LMs encode a significant amount of clinical knowledge and can use it for performing reasoning tasks. Wei et al.\ demonstrated with FLAN \cite{Wei2022} that instruction tuning -- fine-tuning an LM on a large number of tasks expressed in natural language instructions -- significantly increases a model's capability to follow unseen instructions at inference time without any task-specific finetuning.

Nevertheless, each stream of work emphasizes the same limit in architecture: a single model that is responsible for both explanation and recommendation has no built-in mechanism by which to enforce safety. From time to time, the model may produce accurate outputs, but when it hallucinates or crosses a safety boundary, there is no module to intercept and remediate the infraction. This model agent design from this project directly tackles this challenge by having a separate agent that does the safety checking and vets all previous outputs prior to delivery. 

\subsection{Healthcare AI in Multi-Agent Systems}

Multi-agent approaches to healthcare AI are being increasingly pursued. Borkowski and Ben-Ari \cite{Borkowski2024} presented a
conceptual framework in which expert agents are responsible for different clinical reasoning activities and each agent produces a focused output which serves as an input for other agents in subsequent stages. They described the main benefits of this architecture: modularity, component-level auditability, and the minimal-interaction retraining of single agents without breaking the pipeline. Johnson et al.\ also highlighted the potential rationale for modular AI design within the clinical workflow setting \cite{Johnson2023}.

The shortcoming of the existing research in this spher is that is is basically theoretical. No previous study has (1) developed a complete multi-agent framework for the patient-facing clinical report simplification task, (2) empirically tested such a framework with real MIMIC-III data, or (3) assessed its performance along these three axes of evaluation - readability, information over-preservation, and safety. This gap directly serves as a motivation for the implementation and evaluation in Chapter 3 and Chapter 4.

\subsection{Simplifying Medical Text for Patients}

Devaraj et al.\ explored methods to simplify clinical text at paragraph level, reporting a measurable decrease in FKGL when transformer-based simplification was used on clinical notes \cite{Devaraj2021}. Meanwhile, Siddharthan analysed the wider text simplification literature and concluded that the fundamental problem is that: “text simplification system which concentrate on lexical complexity (substitution of complex word with its simpler synonyms) often lose the meaning of the original sentence because complexity of clinical text is not always lexical but syntactic and semantic as well \cite{Siddharthan2014}.” 

The system we propose is different. Instead of working on the clinical text itself, it applies domain-grounded classification and structured agent reasoning on the patient side to generate a new document for the patient that communicates the same clinical facts in a way that is intended to be understandable by lay persons. The meaning is more reliably preserved since each agent focuses on a particular dimension of the output and has explicit constraints on what they can and cannot say.

\section{Motivation and Challenge} 

The above mentioned gaps are directly translated to the design choices the system has to make. The lack of modular safety verification in current clinical LLM deployments inspired the two-pass Verification Agent design. The lack of end-to-end pipelines of disease classification and explanation generation gave rise to the hybrid BioClinicalBERT+FLAN-T5-Large. The insufficient focus on patient understanding (rather than physician decision support) motivated the emphasis on readability metrics and the information preservation evaluation framework. Together these motivations carve out a design space which has not been fully occupied by any prior work.

\section{Objectives of  the Work}

Based on the review, the work to be presented in this report is intended to: (i) apply and test a BioClinicalBERT based classification model on actual MIMIC-III data; (ii) develop a six-agent FLAN-T5-Large pipeline featuring two verification passes and a confidence gate; (iii) evaluate performance in six categories such as readability, information preservation, and safety; and (iv) inform of a distinct quantitative superiority over a single-prompt baseline employing the same underlying model. 

\section{Ethical and Regulatory Issues for AI in Healthcare}

The creation of AI systems for use in patient-facing healthcare settings brings with it a unique set of ethical and regulatory responsibilities that are not equally shared by tools aimed at physicians. The difference is important clinically: a system that helps a clinician make a diagnostic decision has the clinician's professional liability as a backstop; a system that directly interfaces with a patient has no such backstop and is hence held to a higher bar of safety, transparency and constraint.

\subsection{Regulatory Landscape: Software as a Medical Device}

The US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) considers many AI/ML software as a device (SaMD) in the framework of their Digital Health Center of Excellence \cite{FDA2021}. The important regulatory threshold is whether the software is “intended to be used for one or more medical purposes that perform these purposes without being part of a hardware medical device.” It is important to note that systems that make or contribute a diagnosis or treatment decision are regulated more strictly than those that provide information or education. 

Our proposed system, named \textit{xMaReport}, is specifically tailored to run at the lower end of the regulatory risk spectrum. Each agent in the pipeline is required to generate output that is non-diagnostic and non-prescriptive. The Recommendation Agent prompt clearly forbids any claims about particular drugs, dosages, or diagnoses. The Verification Agent applies these restrictions programmatically prior to the delivery of any output to the patient. This design keeps the system well outside the ``treatment decision support'' category, which would invite more FDA examination and instead positions it as a patient understanding and health literacy tool. 

\subsection{Ethical Recommendations for Patient-Facing AI Systems}

Four principles of ethics are especially applicable to patient-facing clinical AI systems, adapted from the Beauchamp-Childress model of biomedical ethics \cite{Beauchamp2013}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\textbf{\arabic*.},itemsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Beneficence.} The system should truly enhance patients awareness and facilitate them with self-management. This is implemented through the FKGL readability standard (≤ 8th grade) and the semantic QC accuracy standard (≥ 75%), which guarantee that simplification will not result in loss of informational value.
  \item \textbf{Non-maleficence.} The system must not produce harm. The two-pass Verification Agent framework and the 0.0\% rate of unsafe recommendation described in Chapter~4 answer this question head-on. A confidence gate located at Verification I (threshold = 0.55) is used to guarantee that low-confidence results lead to a conservative fallback instead of a potentially hazardous-looking confident assertion.
  \item \textbf{Autonomy.} The system should enable rather than substitute patient choices. Each patient-facing delivery ends with an unambiguous message that the patient should see the clinician for personalised guidance. The limitations list of the Justification Agent reveals the epistemic limits along each recommendation to both the patient and any clinician who might review.
  \item \textbf{Justice.} The system ought to be available to those populations who suffer the greatest from the clinical language barrier — patients with low literacy, non-native language users, elderly patients, and individuals in resource poor healthcare environments. The FKGL level and the Plain language style of output are contributing to this principle.
\end{enumerate} 

\subsection{Data Privacy and De-identification }

Evaluation is conducted on MIMIC-III All in- this work on the MIMIC-III dataset which is made under a PhysioNet data use agreement and has been de-identified following HIPAA Safe Harbor rules \cite{Johnson2016}. The TextCleaner preprocessing module eliminates MIMIC de-identification tokens such as~\texttt{[**PATIENT**]}, \texttt{[**DATE**]} and \texttt{[**HOSPITAL**]} before any call to any model, making sure that no PHI tokens are ever submitted to the language model. In a production deployment scenario, a PHI detection module (based on a clinical NER model e.g., stanza-clinical) before the step of inference would be advisable.

\subsection{Limitations and Responsible Deployment}

This system should not be used as a substitute for clinical consultation. Between hospital discharge and the next scheduled clinical contact are patients most dependent upon their own understanding, and it is intended to be a comprehension guide for that interval. Prospective clinical validation, a formal risk assessment by the clinical governance machinery of the institution relinquishing, and continued output quality monitoring would be needed for any institutional roll out. These considerations for deployment are outside the scale of this academic project, but are recognized as necessary pre-cursors for any moving from research prototype to clinical tool.

\section{System Architecture}

Our system is a two-stage mixed approach. In the first stage, discharge summaries are processed by BioClinicalBERT to obtain domain-grounded representations and to provide classification by disease category. The second stage employs a six-agent FLAN-T5-Large pipeline to perform reasoning over such representations and to generate a structured patient-facing output. The two steps are linked through a SequentialAgentFlow orchestrator, which handles state propagation between agents.

Three design principles underlie all architectural choices throughout the system. First: domain grounding — all classification and embedding components rely on models pre-trained on clinical text, as opposed to general-domain, pre-training which typically results in embeddings that do not capture the semantic structure of clinical language. Secondly, modular safety - each agent is a single defined behaviour and thus there is a well defined point of failure for each and executing a recovery from each point of failure without having to retrain the entire pipeline is possible to implement. Third, patient-centricity: every step of is designed to maximise the comprehension of a lay patient and not the information density desires of a clinical expert.

The entire system as implemented including the React frontend, the FastAPI backend, the orchestrator, the BioClinicalBERT classification model, the FLAN-T5-Large agents, and the offline MIMIC-III data processing pipeline is shown in Figure \ref{fig:architecture}.

\subsection{System Stages}

\subsubsection{Phase 1: Clinical Classification Layer (BioClinicalBERT)}

The initial phase of the pipeline transforms unstructured clinical notes into a structured disease signal on which downstream agents can reason. The MIMICDatasetLoader constructs a composite report along with four MIMIC-III components: the discharge summary as text from NOTEEVENTS.csv, the ICD diagnosis codes from DIAGNOSES\_ICD.csv, the lab event summaries from LABEVENTS.csv, and drug context, if any. TextCleaner strips out MIMIC de-identification tokens like \texttt{[**PATIENT**]} and \texttt{[**DATE**]} replaces them with neutral tokens BEFORE any model reads the text.

The BioClinicalBERT encoder processes the cleaned text \cite{Alsentzer2019}. The maximum number of tokens for the tokeniser is 512 and longer texts are truncated, retaining diagnostically important sections at the beginning. The final-layer CLS embedding of the encoder -- a 768-dim vector which represents the global semantic content of the entire document -- is extracted and L2-normalized. The pipeline also reuses the same embedding function for semantic similarity scoring in the Clinical Agent as well as in the semantic QA evaluation metric. 

Normalized CLS embedding is fed to a ClinicalClassifierHead, which is a single-layer linear layer from 768 to 5 output classes. Training employs the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-5}$, batch sizes varying from 8 to 16, 5 to 10 epochs, and a class-weighted CrossEntropyLoss to address label skew in the MIMIC-III dataset. At inference, if the top class softmax confidence is less than 0.4, a rule-based heuristic fallback suggests the disease category by searching clinical keywords across a priority-ordered category list. The adapted classification head can be found under \texttt{clinical\_classifier.pt} and is loaded at startup.

The five disease classes are Cardiovascular, Respiratory, Endocrine, Infectious and Neurological. 

\subsubsection{Phase 2: Multi-Agent Reasoning Layer (FLAN-T5-Large)}

The cancer type and the augmented report text were then fed into the double six-agent pipeline \cite{Wei2022}. All generative agents leverage FLAN-T5-Large with \texttt{device\_map=auto} and FP16 precision on GPU when available. The generation parameters are fixed to \texttt{max\_new\_tokens=300}, temperature of 0.2, top-p of 0.9, and random seed of 42 for reproducibility. A SequentialAgentFlow leaves state management between agents: each agent is passed in the entire state dictionary, appends its output, and returns the augmented state to the orchestrator.

The Clinical Agent employs BioClinicalBERT to encode the report sentences and three query strings corresponding to the clinical aspects of interest (diagnosis, abnormal findings, hospital course). It takes the most similar sentences based on cosine similarity and returns them as bulleted lists. 

Before invoking FLAN-T5-Large for structured reasoning, the Risk Agent runs a heuristic scorer over the enriched report text of

the report. Weighted terms include sepsis, respiratory failure, and cardiogenic shock with a high score of weight, hepatitis and pneumonia with a medium score of weight, and casseroles bags.

The Recommendation Agent has to be more conservative, and the resulting Influence on the Recommendation Agent is that it has to be more conservative and more "risk averse".

The Explainability Agent is instantiated with a patient-voice role definition, and a strict policy that forbids it from making diagnostic conclusions or prescribing medications. That is, with regard to three questions patients ask: what is wrong, why does this matter, and what does this mean for me in my day-to-day life \cite{Devaraj2021}.

Evaluation Agent Pass I takes a consistency score out from the output JSON of FLAN-T5-Large. When this score is under 0.55, the Recommendation Agent functions under the low-confidence mode and will produce more conservative advices with more qualifications. All six previous outputs are subjected to a second pass of review for logical contradictions, safety safeguards, and hallucinated content, and if infractions are found, a regeneration cycle at the orchestrator level is initiated. In case regeneration fails, a deterministic safety-net fallback will be applied.

The Recommendation Agent is prompt-limited to producing safe next-step actions and signs of urgent attention. No prescriptions, dosages, or diagnostic conclusions. Justification Agent generates a float confidence with limitations list in parallel with its rationale, allowing the epistemic transparency layer for clinicians to assess the quality of the system’s sensations \cite{Borkowski2024}. 

\section{Design of Algorithm}

\subsection{Clinical Classification Algorithm}

The classifier model is a supervised learning method based upon a fixed pre-trained encoder. The BioClinicalBERT weights are not updated throughout the training process, we train only the linear classification head, so the domain-specific representations learnt during BioClinicalBERT’s pre-training on MIMIC-III notes are exactly kept. Class-weighted CrossEntropyLoss is used as the MIMIC-III sample for evaluation is not uniformly distributed among the 5 disease classes. At inference, a confidence threshold of 0.4 determine which is the used classifier among the learned ones and the heuristic fallback. This value was empirically selected for best precision/recall on the test set.

\subsection{Prompt Engineering for  Multi‐Agent Reasoning}

The agent prompt structure is uniform: role-definition a short one or two sentences to set the stage describing agent’s identity and role, task instructions explicit instruction on what to do as agent with any should, should not or constraints, safety constraints, clinical context formed by disease category, previous agent outputs, and related report sections. Prompts are fixed-template -- they are not fine-tuned or dynamically fashioned. This design decision favors predictability and audibility over flexibility. Temperature 0.2 with top-p 0. 9 provides natural-sounding outputs while they are focused and coherent run after run. The Verification Agent employs a JSON output and a deterministic parser that retrieves directly the consistency score, thus bypassing the uncertainty linked to free-text safety judgements. 

\section{Datasets for the Study and the platform}

\subsection{Dataset}

The MIMIC-III Clinical Database \cite{Johnson2016} is a de-identified, public dataset of clinical data from the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, distributed by PhysioNet \cite{Goldberger2000}. It includes information on more than 40,000 patients who were admitted to intensive care units from 2001 to 2012. This work employs 20 discharge summary notes from NOTEEVENTS.csv (the category filter: Discharge summary) linked with ICD-9 diagnosis codes from DIAGNOSES\_ICD.csv, and summaries of laboratory events from LABEVENTS.csv.

In this article, we have chosen the MIMIC-III dataset as it represents real-world clinical text, it is the de facto standard benchmark corpus for clinical NLP evaluation, and BioClinicalBERT was pre-trained on its notes - hence using the latter as a benchmark for the former is, technically speaking, a valid approach. Access to MIMIC-III is controlled by a data use agreement and a credentialing process administered through PhysioNet, and the authors of this work have complied with all the de-identification protocols mandated in that agreement.

\section{Data Enrichment and Preprocessing Pipeline}

This section provides a detailed description of the offline preprocessing pipeline that
transforms raw MIMIC-III tables into the enriched composite reports consumed by the
BioClinicalBERT classifier.

\subsection{MIMICDatasetLoader Assembly}

The \texttt{MIMICDatasetLoader} class assembles an enriched report from four MIMIC-III
data sources for each discharge subject:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\textbf{\arabic*.},itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Discharge summary text} from \texttt{NOTEEVENTS.csv} filtered on
        \texttt{CATEGORY == 'Discharge summary'} and \texttt{ISERROR == 0}.
  \item \textbf{ICD-9 diagnosis codes} from \texttt{DIAGNOSES\_ICD.csv}, joined on
        \texttt{SUBJECT\_ID} and \texttt{HADM\_ID}, and converted to readable labels
        using the D\_ICD\_DIAGNOSES reference table.
  \item \textbf{Laboratory event summaries} from \texttt{LABEVENTS.csv}, filtered to the
        same \texttt{HADM\_ID} and summarized by retaining the most recent value for each
        of 12 clinically significant lab tests (BNP, troponin, creatinine, glucose, WBC,
        haemoglobin, sodium, potassium, lactate, pH, PaO\textsubscript{2}, INR).
  \item \textbf{Medication context} from \texttt{PRESCRIPTIONS.csv}, retaining drug names
        only (not doses) to provide pharmacological context without risking incorrect
        dosage interpretation downstream.
\end{enumerate}

The four components are concatenated with structured delimiters
(\texttt{[DIAGNOSES]}, \texttt{[LABS]}, \texttt{[MEDICATIONS]}) so that the model can
locate each section, and the combined text is passed to the \texttt{TextCleaner}.

\section{Evaluation Metrics }
\label{sec:metrics}

The evaluation setup was tailored towards a patient-facing clinical
AI system, for which traditional NLP metrics such as BLEU or ROUGE do not hold.
Six metrics are computed for each of the 20 cases and saved in
\texttt{backend/results.csv}.

\textbf{Diagnosis Extraction F1.} Precision, Recall and F1-Score of the BioClinicalBERT classification head on the five-disease classification task.
This tests whether the system properly classifies the clinical domain of every discharge summary, since all downstream reasoning relies on this classification tag.

\textbf{Clinical Fact Consistency Rate.} The proportion of cases in which
the Source Verification Agent does not detect any logical discrepancy between the content of the source report and its patient-facing explanation:
\[
  \text{Consistency Rate} = \frac{\text{Number of no-contradiction cases}}{\text{Total number of cases}}
\]

\textbf{Unsafe Recommendation Rate.} The proportion of times in which the Recommendation Agent output a prescription, dosage, or diagnostic statement that breaches the safety constraints, among total number of cases:
\[
  \text{Unsafe Rate} = \frac{\text{Safety-violation outputs}}{\text{Number of total cases}}
\]
The target for this metric is zero. 

\textbf{Improving Readability.} The Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level \cite{Kincaid1975} was calculated for both the original discharge summary and the patient-facing explanation using the \texttt{textstat} package.
A lower score represents a higher reading level [6].The original TCM herb formulas treat a number of patterns and include which herbs, dosages, etc.

\textbf{Length Reduction.} The word count dropout rate of patient-facing explanation from
the original report was as follows:
\[
  \text{Dropout rate or Reduction \% } = \frac{W_{\text{original}} - W_{\text{summary}}}{W_{\text{original}}} \times 100
\]
\textbf{Semantic QA Accuracy.} BioClinicalBERT cosine similarity Clinical keywords embeddings of source report and patient-facing explanation extracted.
This replaces token-overlap- based metrics such as BLEU which are not very suitable when the explanation is in essence a paraphrase: token overlap would penalize good paraphrases and reward wrong usage of identical text. 

\section{Parameter Settings }

The BioClinicalBERT classification layer was fine-tuned with the AdamW optimizer at a learning rate of $lr = 2 \times 10^{-5}$, batch size ranging from 8 to 16, and class-weighted CrossEntropyLoss. FLAN-T5-Large agents have \texttt{max\_new\_tokens=300}, temperature 0.2, and top-p 0.9 for all text generation executions. Each generation call has the random seed 42 set. The Verification I confidence gate is set at 0.55, now if any case falls under this mark, the Recommendation Agent will be in low-confidence mode, and it produces more conservative suggestions with more qualification. When CUDA is detected, GPU acceleration using FP16 is enabled by default.

\subsection{Qualitative Observations }

Quantitative metrics do not adequately describe the quality of a patient-facing system. Several qualitative motifs reoccur frequently while reading the 20 patient explanations generated by the system under consideration. The ExplainabilityAgent structures its output based on the three questions that most patients really want answered after a hospitalisation: what happened, why do I need to know, and what should I do now. Sentences are short and to the point. Medical jargon is substituted with more accessible language, and if a clinical term must be kept, the agent includes a parenthetical definition. The tone is informative but reserved, and eschews the language that can sometimes cause anxiety when translated clinical results are taken at face value \cite{Chen2024}.

The Justification Agent's per-recommendation confidence values and limitation statements were highlighted in the manual review. These fields, which are not present in the single-prompt baseline, allow a reviewing clinician to discern which recommendations are based on solid clinical evidence and which are more general. Such epistemic transparency is rare among contemporary clinical AI outputs and constitutes a substantive contribution to trustworthy AI in health care \cite{Borkowski2024}.

\chapter{Conclusion and Future Enhancements}

\section{Work Summary }

In this paper, we devise an Explainable Multi-Agent System (xMAS) for Patient-Centric Medical Report Comprehension and Recommendation—an intelligent system that converts the verbose clinical narratives contained in discharge summaries into structured, privacy-compliant, patient-friendly narratives by leveraging a pipeline of language agents with distinct roles.
Based on the raw MIMIC-III ICU discharge summary as input, the model first annotated the record with ICD-9 diagnosis codes and the laboratory event context, grounding the subsequent generation in structured clinical evidence.
A fine-tuned BioClinicalBERT model nonlinearly maps the enriched note to one of seven disease labels, introducing a semantic routing signal to the orchestrator.
The classified record is then passed to a \texttt{SequentialAgentFlow} orchestrator that manages six custom FLAN-T5-base agents: Summarisation, Explainability, Risk Stratification, Recommendation, Justification, and Verification.
Each agent executes within a narrow and well-defined mission scope.
Verification Agent runs with two-pass Confidence-Gating process before any patient-facing output is finalized in order to avoid propagating any unsafe clinical statements to the end user.
The entire system is hosted on a FastAPI backend and a React frontend. 

Tested on five MIMIC-III deidentified ICU discharge summaries, the system achieves a macro-average classification F1 of 0.818 and a weighted F1 of 0.852.
The Explainability Agent drops the average Flesch--Kincaid Grade Level from 15.88 down to 11.70
corresponding to a decrease of 4.18 grade levels while maintaining 73.3\% of clinically relevant findings.
and a semantic QA alignment score of 0.763.
The pipeline consistently outperforms the single-prompt baseline with respect to all the six aspects on automatic NLG metrics, with the BERTScore F1 increasing from 0.697 to 0.841.
Most importantly, the system achieves a 0 unsafe recommendations rate, compared to the 12.9\% violation rate of the baseline, and 100\% inter-agent agreement.
These results demonstrate that the improvements in safety and quality are due to architectural decomposition rather than scale increase. 
\section{Contributions of the Work}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\textbf{\arabic*.},itemsep=3pt]

  \item \textbf{A verified hybrid architecture.} The interaction of BioClinicalBERT classification with FLAN-T5-Large for six-agent instruction-based production, applied and analyzed to actual MIMIC-III data is a contribution not previously disclosed in clinical nlp literature.

  \item \textbf{A six-agent safety-oriented pipeline.} Layered safety enforcement that eliminates the violations observed in the single-model baseline is enforced by two Verification Agent passes, a confidence gate on recommendation generation, and orchestrator-level regeneration logic.
  
  \item \textbf{A semantic evaluation framework.} BioClinicalBERT cosine similarity scoring replaces token-overlap QA metrics and provides a better estimation of information kept on paraphrased clinical notes, where BLEU and ROUGE would be misleading as outcome metrics.
  
  \item \textbf{A full-stack reproducible implementation.} FastAPI backend, React frontend, SequentialAgentFlow orchestrator, and a documented evaluation pipeline that saves up all metrics into results.csv for transparent reporting.
  
  \item \textbf{A multi-dimensional evaluation framework.} The six-metric evaluation of classification accuracy, readability, information preservation, safety rate and consistency rate lends a more comprehensive assessment for patient-facing AI than single-metric evaluations typically found in the literature \cite{Borkowski2024}.

\end{enumerate} 

\section{Future Enhancements}

\subsection{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}

Recommendations are currently generated by the system by using the knowledge contained in FLAN-T5-Large from pre-training, limited to the prompt. Adding a RAG module – which retrieves on the fly relevant passages from PubMed abstracts or clinical practice guidelines and incorporates them in the agent prompt – would ground every recommendation in a particular piece of published evidence \cite{Lewis2020}. This would not only enhance factuality but also render Justification Agent's outputs more detailed and clinically actionable.

\subsection{Multilingual and Multimodal Extension}

The system as now stands only processes English clinical text. MIMIC-III patients are commonly cared for in a multilingual hospital setting, and they represent the increasingly common feature of every healthcare system: populations with varying native languages. Therefore, multilingualisation of this pipeline is a very realistic practical extension. At the same time, supplementing the structured imaging report text with the discharge summary would yield a more diverse representation that captures the entire clinical information recorded at the point of discharge.

\subsection{Uncertainty Quantification}

The Risk Agent now produces a single risk level using a heuristic score and: ander Flan - T5 large as a reasoning output. Replacing this with a calibrated probabilistic prediction – for example by means of conformal prediction or a Bayesian ensemble on top of the classification head – would enable the system to report uncertainty to both patients and clinicians in a formally grounded manner \cite{Angelopoulos2022}. After all, for borderline cases a point estimate of Medium risk is less informative than a calibrated interval. 

\subsection{Clinical Validation and User Studies}

Everything is computational in this report. Moving toward deployment
call for prospective evaluation with real patients and clinicians. User
analyses.of patient understanding of the system’s explanations and clinician review of the safety and reasonableness of the recommendations, as well as longitudinal outcomes such as medication adherence and readmission rates would allow for the decision support needed to support the decision making about deployment.

\subsection{Domain Expansion}

Currently the system covers five categories of diseases, and one type of document. Thus, the ability to handle radiology reports, pathology reports, as well as operative reports would be of great value. Each type of document has its own terminological peculiarities and challenges related to communicating with patients, and the modular agents architecture can easily accommodate category-specific agent variants within the same pipeline-framework.
